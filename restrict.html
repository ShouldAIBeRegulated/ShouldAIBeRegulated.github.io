Yes, Let's Restrict AI Development.

Limitations need to be established.

There are a few ways to look at the state of AI development. Growth of this proportion will almost always prompt a response, be it from the private or public sector. In that sense, a great deal of people have agreed on two points. First, the state of regulation surrounding AI is clearly lacking. Second, we need to work proactively and swiftly to remedy this. Here’s a look into this side of the debate.

In fact, this field grew so quickly, expanding far outside of its origin in niche computer science, that it garnered the attention of the government. Currently, legislation surrounding computers in general is flimsy and vague, but that looks like it’s on track to change.

In September of 2018, a group of senators has pushed the government to enact legislation legislation on the federal level that will open the doors for more comprehensive regulation of AI technology. The purpose of this bill is to promote US competitiveness in the new field, but to also serve to protect us from the potential risks associated with such powerful technology. It’s clear that the attitude towards the regulation of AI is taking a favorable turn with the development of this legislation.

U.S. Senate receiving an AI briefing. NBC News.

It’s not just the government that is having thoughts about restricting AI, however. In fact, the government seems to be one of the late actors on this issue. Industry experts and researchers have been in talks about possible restrictions on AI tech for years. An example would be Microsoft Research Scholar Kate Crawford, who warns of the inevitability of AI bias. In essence, we cannot truly take what an AI says as truth- it’s simply irrational to assume an AI is always correct.

As powerful and accurate as AI technology will become, issues will certainly arise if we allow it to be used for purposes such as deciding between two people to kill in a car accident , or deciding whether a criminal is likely to commit a crime again. As such technologies slowly become a reality, this truth becomes more and more important for us to understand. The ‘TheNextWeb’ article linked above mentions that we will have to accept that our new technology will certainly fail a few times- that is, we won’t get it perfect the first time around. However, it’s probably better for regulations to decide when it’s appropriate to test technology that will surely have inaccuracies. As long as there is error and bias in AI, which at this moment is inevitable, using it for processes of magnitude as great as deciding a human’s fate may be too much for now, and should therefore at least be regulated.

Some people in the corporate world have also expressed their concern for the continued development of AI tech. Specifically, Elon Musk has taken a very clear stance on the issue. Not only has he invested heavily in one of the fastest growing AI research think tanks in the world, but he claims to have only done so to ‘keep an eye’ on such technology. Since then, he’s taken steps to carefully integrate AI into his other companies, Tesla and SpaceX.

Musk in an interview about AI. BusinessIndsider.

He believes that the government ‘steps in after too many bad things happen,’ and that instead of looking at the benefits of AI, we should focus on taking proactive steps to implement solutions to the massive moral and ethical issues that the cutting age of AI will face.

Overall, the frightening pace of AI development has inspired many to take this middleground path; not to let it free or stop it entirely, but to instead proceed with caution and act proactively to prevent disaster.
