We really shouldn't get there in the first place.

We're better off without this technology.

Despite the fact that many welcome the never-ending stream of innovation that is AI technology, there are still those who would value a break. What some may call an astronomical amount of progress, others may call worrisome. As our technology rapidly advances, the complementary political and moral infrastructure that we have must adjust to keep up. Some hold the opinion that this sort of unprecedented growth requires a major restructuring to the way we think, or that we shouldn’t even continue advancement at all, for our own good.

Once again, a CEO dictates the attitude of the company under them. In that sense, CEO Tim Cook of Apple is known for attention to detail and consideration of ‘the little things’. In many ways, Apple is viewed as a perfectionist, refined company. For that very same reason, Cook claims that he simply dislikes the direction his company is going in terms of integrating AI, and that he doesn’t want to talk about it.

Tim Cook, CEO of Apple. Wccftech.

Although this is a seemingly weak statement for the CEO of one of the world’s largest corporations, make no mistake. This is the plight of a company prey to its market. Inevitably, the market will move towards integrating all of these novel AI technologies. The major issue is, the pace at which this technology is developing is simply inappropriate, because we have not truly understood how to apply the technology.

In addition to what we simply don’t know about the future of AI, we have to consider what is already happening. With the recent spike in development of AI-based job automation, AI technology is having a very real effect on our already established economy. In fact, deployment of job-automation software on even larger scales would consequently lead to unemployment on equally large scales. The resulting wealth disparity could arguably dismantle our economy as we know it, on a national or global scale.

Looking further into the future gives way for even more radical possibilities.

The late professor Stephen Hawking explores this possible future, going so far as to make the claim that when AI development reaches a point where AI can perform all tasks that we as humans have to do to sustain ourselves, we will be faced with a choice- distribute it evenly and prosper, or allow for our capitalistic society to distribute this wealth in a top-heavy manner.

The late Professor Hawking. ImageSpot.

The latter choice, which Professor Hawking was certain we would end up making, would usher in an age of the first trillionaires and simultaneously, poverty and famine on an unimaginable scale. In other words, Hawking was convinced that AI would truly end civilation as we know it.

There are essentially two schools of thought in terms of stopping AI development in its tracks. The first school of thought, demonstrated by Apple CEO Tim Cook, is backed by the idea that we simply do not understand it, and we would need to stop advancement and take some time to evaluate the future before continuing development. Another school of thought is Hawking’s even more pessimistic point of view, directly opposite of Satya Nadella’s point of view in the previous article: the way our society works today guarantees that the inception of a super-intelligent AI would lead to our demise.

A tech-driven dystopia. Unknown Artist - Imgur.

Either way, in the words of Ian Malcolm from Jurassic Park, it’s clear that we as a society were so engrossed with the idea that we could, we didn’t stop to think if we should in the first place.
